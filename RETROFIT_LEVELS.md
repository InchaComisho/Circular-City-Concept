# Circular City Concept Retrofit Levels
## A Level 0 to Level 5 Framework for Existing-City Transformation

---

## Purpose of This Document

This document organizes the transition from present linear cities to a Circular City OS as Levels 0 through 5. Each level clarifies who can act, approximate difficulty, first actions, and what not to do.

---

## Level Comparison Table

| Level | State | Main Circulation | Actor | Difficulty |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Level 0 | Current linear city | Limited | All actors | Current state |
| Level 1 | Household and small-facility circularity | Rainwater, organic matter, emergency water | Individuals and facilities | Low |
| Level 2 | Building-scale circulation | Greywater, greening, drainage heat, sensors | Owners associations and operators | Medium |
| Level 3 | District-scale circular infrastructure | Reclaimed water, rain gardens, urban farms, batteries | Districts and municipalities | Medium to high |
| Level 4 | Municipal circular infrastructure | Treated wastewater, public facilities, disaster water networks | Municipalities | High |
| Level 5 | Circular City OS | Integrated water, heat, energy, food, organic matter, and disaster resilience | Municipalities, citizens, operators | Highest |

---

## Level 0: Current Linear City

Rainwater is drained away, wastewater is discharged after treatment, organic matter is mostly incinerated, buildings mostly consume energy, and reuse is limited.

The first action is to visualize current flows of water, heat, electricity, organic matter, and disaster functions. What not to do: install expensive equipment without understanding the existing system.

---

## Level 1: Household and Small-Facility Circularity

This level begins with rainwater tanks, non-potable use, balcony and rooftop greening, composting, and emergency water.

| Item | Content |
| --- | --- |
| Actors | Detached houses, small shops, parts of schools |
| First actions | Rainwater tanks, irrigation, cleaning, emergency water |
| Transition requirements | Accidental-drinking prevention, cleaning, maintenance responsibility |
| What not to do | Convert rainwater to drinking water or create unmanaged compost |

---

## Level 2: Building-Scale Circulation

Apartments, offices, and commercial facilities introduce rainwater storage, greywater reuse, rooftop greening, solar integration, drainage heat recovery, and basic sensors.

| Item | Content |
| --- | --- |
| Actors | Owners associations, building managers, operators |
| First actions | Shared rainwater use, toilet flushing, planting irrigation |
| Transition requirements | Separated piping, water-quality testing, building rules, maintenance contracts |
| What not to do | Install intermediate-water systems without clear management responsibility |

---

## Level 3: District-Scale Circular Infrastructure

Districts combine reclaimed-water piping, rain gardens, permeable pavements, mist cooling, urban farms, distributed batteries, and organic-matter collection.

| Item | Content |
| --- | --- |
| Actors | District managers, neighborhood groups, developers, municipalities |
| First actions | Rain gardens, public heat countermeasures, organic-matter collection |
| Transition requirements | Resident agreement, cost allocation, water-quality and hygiene management |
| What not to do | Deploy mist or reclaimed water in crowded places without validation |

---

## Level 4: Municipal Circular Infrastructure

Municipalities advance wastewater reuse, treated wastewater for public uses, wastewater heat recovery, public facility BIPV, disaster water networks, and integrated water, heat, and energy data.

| Item | Content |
| --- | --- |
| Actors | Municipalities, water and sewerage departments, public facility managers |
| First actions | Public facility demonstrations, circular evacuation shelters, non-potable treated wastewater use |
| Transition requirements | Ordinances, audits, third-party review, public data |
| What not to do | Advance potable reuse without public agreement |

---

## Level 5: Circular City OS

AI-assisted circulation management integrates water, heat, energy, food, organic matter, and disaster resilience. It includes adaptive maintenance, public transparency, and circular governance.

| Item | Content |
| --- | --- |
| Actors | Municipalities, citizens, operators, research institutions |
| First actions | Data standardization and clear separation between manual operation and AI support |
| Transition requirements | Transparency, audits, explainability, manual fallback |
| What not to do | Treat AI as the final responsible authority |

---

## Transition Requirements

Moving between levels depends not on the number of devices, but on maturity in safety, maintenance, agreement, governance, and data.

| Transition | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| 0 to 1 | Small non-potable circulation and maintenance habits |
| 1 to 2 | Building management, separated piping, water-quality confirmation |
| 2 to 3 | District agreement, cost allocation, public-space safety management |
| 3 to 4 | Municipal governance, audits, public infrastructure operation |
| 4 to 5 | Data integration, transparency, and separation of AI support from human judgment |

---

## Summary

A circular city does not begin at Level 5. It begins with Level 1 rainwater, non-potable use, greening, emergency water, and maintenance records. The more levels are skipped, the more management failure becomes a risk rather than the technology itself.
