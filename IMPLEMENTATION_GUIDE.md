# Circular City Concept Implementation Guide
## A Phased Introduction Model for Houses, Apartments, Buildings, Schools, Hospitals, Districts, and Municipalities

---

## 1. Purpose of This Document

This document provides implementation-oriented guidance for introducing the Circular City Concept into existing cities in stages. While the main README organizes the overall concept and technical structure, this guide clarifies what houses, apartments, buildings, schools, hospitals, districts, and municipalities can introduce first, and what should be handled cautiously.

This is not a completed construction manual. It is an implementation framework that assumes validation of water quality, hygiene, architecture, electricity, fire safety, urban planning, public agreement, and maintenance.

---

## 2. Basic Implementation Principles

Circular-city implementation should not begin with potable reuse or a large citywide OS. The first entry points are rainwater storage, non-potable use, greening, emergency water, cleaning, and irrigation.

| Principle | Content | Caution |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Start small | Begin at household and facility scale | Do not assume citywide integration at the beginning |
| Begin with non-potable uses | Prioritize toilets, irrigation, cleaning, cooling, and emergency water | Potable reuse requires a different level of validation |
| Separate water quality by use | Match water quality to each use | Prevent cross-connections and accidental drinking |
| Make maintenance mandatory | Include cleaning, inspection, replacement, and records in the design | Circularity without maintenance becomes risk |
| Distinguish demonstration technologies | Treat small hydropower, urban wind, and perovskite solar as site-dependent or demonstration-stage elements | Do not assume excessive power output |

---

## 3. Detached House Model

Detached houses should begin with rainwater tanks, garden and balcony irrigation, cleaning water, and emergency water. Roof area, gutters, installation location, overturn prevention, freezing, mosquitoes, algae, and accidental-drinking prevention must be checked.

| Component | Early Introduction | Cautious Introduction | Mandatory Management |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rainwater tank | Recommended | Do not convert to drinking use | First flush, cleaning, labeling |
| Garden and balcony greening | Recommended | Heavy planters | Drainage, pests, watering |
| Household composting | Conditionally recommended | Meat, oil, and salty waste | Odor, pests, maturity |
| Small solar and batteries | Conditionally recommended | Uncertified equipment | Electrical safety, fire safety |

---

## 4. Apartment Model

In apartments, implementation depends on agreement among the owners association, property manager, and equipment contractors rather than on individual households alone. Rooftop rainwater storage, intermediate-water use, shared cleaning water, planting irrigation, emergency water, and shared batteries can be examined first.

| Item | Implementation Unit | Items to Confirm |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rainwater storage | Shared building system | Structural load, waterproofing, pumps, outage operation |
| Intermediate water and greywater | Building equipment | Separated piping, odor, hygiene standards, maintenance contract |
| Rooftop greening | Shared areas | Load, waterproofing, wind, evacuation routes |
| Sensor management | Management room or cloud | Water level, water quality, leakage, inspection records |

---

## 5. Office Building and Commercial Facility Model

Office buildings and commercial facilities should connect circular systems with cleaning, toilet flushing, air conditioning, rooftop and exterior greening, and drainage heat recovery. Because many people use these buildings, labeling, accidental-drinking prevention, responsibility boundaries, and periodic inspection must be explicit.

| Priority Area | Example | Main Risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Non-potable water | Toilets, cleaning, irrigation | Cross-connection, water quality decline |
| Heat use | Drainage heat, wastewater heat, air-conditioning waste heat recovery | Cost effectiveness, maintainability |
| Power-generating surfaces | BIPV and demonstration perovskite solar | Fire safety, durability, recovery systems |
| Cooling | Shade, greening, mist | Legionella, slippery surfaces, humidity |

---

## 6. School, Hospital, and Evacuation Shelter Model

Schools, hospitals, and evacuation shelters are important facilities for connecting everyday environmental education and resource saving with disaster continuity. Hospitals require especially strict hygiene, so medical and patient areas must be clearly separated from non-potable circulation systems.

| Facility | Priority Introduction | Disaster Function | Caution |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| School | Rainwater, greening, education, emergency water | Non-drinking living water for toilets and cleaning | Prevent children from accidental drinking |
| Hospital | Rainwater and reclaimed water in non-medical areas, batteries | Minimum power for critical equipment | Infection control, legal compliance |
| Evacuation shelter | Stored water, solar, batteries, simple cooling | Water, power, and heat-risk countermeasures | Operational drills |

---

## 7. District Model

At district scale, multiple buildings can be linked through rain gardens, permeable pavements, reclaimed-water piping, mist cooling, urban farms, organic-matter collection, and distributed batteries. Because ownership is distributed, agreement building and cost allocation become central implementation issues.

| District Function | Example | Management Actor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Water circulation | Rain gardens, permeable pavements, reclaimed-water pipes | Neighborhood groups, owners associations, municipalities |
| Heat adaptation | Shade, mist, water-retentive surfaces | Facility managers, road managers |
| Organic-matter circulation | Composting fallen leaves and pruning residues | Park managers, local groups |
| Data management | Water level, temperature, power, inspection records | Operators, municipalities |

---

## 8. Municipal Model

Municipalities are responsible for reclaimed use of treated wastewater, circular public facilities, disaster water sources, urban data integration, ordinances, subsidies, and demonstration districts. At this level, governance, responsibility, auditing, and transparency are more important than the equipment itself.

---

## 9. Priority Logic

1. Rainwater storage and non-potable use
2. Emergency water and disaster operation
3. Greening, rain gardens, and permeable pavements
4. Building-scale intermediate water and greywater
5. Public-facility and district-scale reclaimed water
6. Drainage heat and wastewater heat recovery
7. Distributed generation and batteries
8. AI and sensors for maintenance support
9. Potable reuse only as a final cautious consideration

---

## 10. What Can Be Introduced and What Requires Caution

| Category | Easier to Introduce | Handle Carefully |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Water | Rainwater for irrigation, cleaning, and emergency water | Potable reuse, reclaimed water for mist |
| Energy | Solar, batteries, drainage heat recovery | Small hydropower, urban wind, full-scale perovskite deployment |
| Organic matter | Fallen leaves, pruning residues, separated food waste | Sludge, mixed waste, salty or oily residues |
| AI | Inspection records, anomaly detection, predictive maintenance | AI-only decisions, opaque automatic control |

---

## 11. Maintenance System

Circularity has value only when maintenance exists. Tanks, pipes, pumps, filters, mist nozzles, batteries, composting areas, greening bases, and sensors can become sources of risk unless they are inspected and recorded.

| Target | Inspection Item | Frequency Logic |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rainwater tank | Sediment, algae, mosquitoes, lid, labels | Seasonally and after heavy rain |
| Intermediate-water equipment | Water quality, odor, piping, pumps | According to laws and design standards |
| Mist equipment | Water quality, nozzles, pipe cleaning | High frequency before and after operation |
| Organic-matter circulation | Temperature, odor, foreign matter, maturity | At each input and before use |
| Sensors | Calibration, communication, abnormal values | Periodic and abnormal-event checks |

---

## 12. Disaster Operation

During disasters, water, power, cooling, toilets, and information can all become scarce at the same time. Circular equipment is not only a resource-saving system for ordinary days; it must also define which functions remain available during emergencies.

| Function | Disaster Role | Preparation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rainwater and emergency water | Cleaning, toilets, fire protection, irrigation | Storage volume, use priority, labels |
| Batteries | Communication, lighting, pumps | Charge maintenance, emergency circuits |
| Greening and mist | Heat refuge support | Water quality and shutdown conditions |
| Sensors | Water level, power, and temperature awareness | Manual operation fallback |

---

## 13. Implementation Checklist

| Check Item | House | Building | District | Municipality |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Starts with non-potable use | Required | Required | Required | Required |
| Prevents accidental drinking and cross-connection | Required | Required | Required | Required |
| Defines maintenance responsibility | Required | Required | Required | Required |
| Includes water-quality and hygiene validation | Conditional | Required | Required | Required |
| Includes disaster operation procedures | Recommended | Required | Required | Required |
| Explains the system to residents and users | Recommended | Required | Required | Required |
| Avoids overstating demonstration technologies | Required | Required | Required | Required |

---

## 14. Summary

Implementing a circular city does not mean building a large ideal city all at once. It begins by adding small, safe circulation systems to existing houses, buildings, schools, hospitals, districts, and municipalities.

The first targets should be rainwater, non-potable water, greening, emergency water, and maintenance. Potable reuse, urban wind, small hydropower, perovskite solar cells, and AI control should be treated as staged introductions that require validation and governance.
