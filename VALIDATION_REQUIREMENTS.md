# Circular City Concept Validation Requirements
## Water Quality, Safety, Governance, Technology Readiness, and Social Acceptance

---

## 1. Purpose

This document organizes the scientific, technical, legal, and social validation items required before implementing the Circular City Concept. The concept is not a proven implementation protocol. Deployment requires confirmation of use-specific water quality, safety, maintenance, responsibility boundaries, and public agreement.

---

## 2. Rainwater Validation

Rainwater use is suitable for early deployment, but it is affected by roofing materials, bird droppings, dust, leaves, first flush, and tank stagnation. Even for non-potable use, first-flush separation, filtration, labeling, cross-connection prevention, and cleaning plans must be confirmed.

## 3. Mist Cooling Hygiene Validation

Mist can be inhaled, so Legionella, general bacteria, biofilm, nozzle contamination, stagnant water, surrounding humidity, and slippery surfaces must be validated. When reclaimed water or rainwater is used, use-specific safety standards, disinfection, abnormal shutdown, and inspection records are mandatory.

## 4. Greywater Validation

Greywater and intermediate water require evaluation of detergents, oils, odor, microorganisms, pipe biofilm, cross-connections, and possible user contact. At building scale, separated piping, color coding, labels, and maintenance contracts are important.

## 5. Reclaimed Water Validation

Reclaimed water and treated wastewater require different water-quality levels depending on use. Irrigation, cleaning, landscape water, industrial water, cooling, and agriculture have different validation requirements and should not be treated as uniformly safe or unsafe.

## 6. Potable Reuse Caution

Potable reuse requires multi-barrier treatment, continuous monitoring, pathogen control, trace chemical control, PFAS, pharmaceuticals, microplastics, legal compatibility, third-party evaluation, and public understanding and acceptance. This concept does not propose potable reuse as an early implementation measure.

## 7. Small Pressure Hydropower Validation

Small pressure hydropower requires validation of flow rate, head, pressure, clogging, pipe resistance, noise, maintenance access, and cost effectiveness. It should be evaluated as auxiliary power for sensors and emergency lights, not as a main urban power source.

## 8. Vertical-Axis Wind Validation

Urban wind is turbulent and unstable. Noise, vibration, falling risks, strong-wind shutdown, bird impacts, resident agreement, and maintainability must be confirmed, beginning with demonstration areas.

## 9. Perovskite Solar Validation

Perovskite solar cells are promising, but long-term durability, encapsulation, fire safety, lead and material management, recovery and recycling, building-code compatibility, and typhoon, snow, and salt resistance must be confirmed. They should not be treated as a completed citywide technology.

## 10. Rooftop and Wall Greening Validation

Structural load, waterproofing, root intrusion, wind, fire safety, pests, irrigation, management after plant death, exterior wall degradation, and evacuation-route impacts must be validated.

## 11. Organic Matter Validation

Organic matter reuse requires confirmation of pathogens, heavy metals, microplastics, salt, odor, pests, pesticides, pharmaceutical residues, foreign materials, fermentation temperature, maturity, and application rate. Natural origin does not automatically mean safe.

## 12. AI and Sensor Validation

AI is a support system, not an infallible authority. Sensor calibration, communication outage, abnormal values, explainability, log retention, manual fallback, and human final judgment must be designed.

## 13. Governance and Responsibility

Responsibility boundaries among owners, managers, contractors, municipalities, and users must be clear. Procedures for water-quality incidents, leakage, outages, equipment failure, accidental drinking, odor, pests, and data failure should be defined in advance.

## 14. Social Acceptance

Residents may have psychological resistance to reclaimed water, organic-matter circulation, AI management, mist, and wind devices. Explanation, labeling, public data, complaint handling, phased deployment, and user choice should be prepared.

---

## 15. Validation Checklist

| Risk | Validation Method | Responsible Actor | Priority | Deployment Stage |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Bacterial growth in rainwater tanks | Water testing and cleaning records | Owner or manager | High | Level 1 |
| Legionella risk in mist | Bacterial testing, disinfection, shutdown conditions | Facility manager | Critical | Level 2-3 |
| Greywater cross-connection | Pipe inspection, color coding, labels | Contractor and manager | Critical | Level 2 |
| Reclaimed water use mismatch | Use-specific standards review | Municipality and operator | High | Level 3-4 |
| Insufficient acceptance of potable reuse | Third-party review and public explanation | Municipality | Critical | Future consideration |
| Organic matter contamination | Heavy metal, pathogen, and foreign-material testing | Operator | High | Level 1-3 |
| AI misjudgment | Logs, manual fallback, audits | Operator | High | Level 4-5 |
| Maintenance failure | Inspection contracts, budget, records | Owner or municipality | Critical | All stages |

---

## 16. Conclusion

The value of the Circular City Concept is not in listing technologies, but in designing circulation that can be safely maintained. Water reuse must be use-specific, and potable reuse requires especially cautious treatment. Maintenance failure can turn circular infrastructure into a source of risk.
